Now, the same thing again with the real, proper terms (so you can sound like you know what you're talking about when you explain it to your kids, your attorney, or anyone technical):
SLIP-39 (Satoshi Labs Improvement Proposal 39) is an open standard created by the Trezor team. It takes your normal BIP-39 seed phrase (the 12- or 24-word recovery phrase that most hardware wallets like Ledger, Arculus, Ellipal, etc. use) and splits it using Shamir's Secret Sharing (often abbreviated SSS or Shamir's Secret Sharing Scheme).
Here's how Shamir's Secret Sharing actually works under the hood (without getting too math-heavy):

Your original seed is treated as a single secret number.
The algorithm creates a random mathematical curve (a polynomial of a certain degree) where the secret is baked into it.
It then generates N shares (the shards). Each share is itself a short mnemonic phrase — usually 20 words long from a special 1,024-word list.
You choose the threshold ahead of time: M-of-N. Example: 3-of-5 means you create 5 total shares, and you need any 3 of them to reconstruct the original seed.

The magic (and the reason it's way better than just chopping your 24 words into chunks):

Information-theoretic security: If you have fewer than M shares, you get literally zero information about the original secret. An attacker with even 2 out of 5 shares cannot start guessing or brute-forcing anything — the math makes it impossible. (Just splitting words leaks entropy and makes the remaining part easier to crack over time.)
Fault tolerance / redundancy: You can lose up to (N – M) shares and still recover everything. Perfect for real life where people lose envelopes, move houses, or pass away.
Each share looks and acts like its own little "mini seed phrase" but is useless alone.
