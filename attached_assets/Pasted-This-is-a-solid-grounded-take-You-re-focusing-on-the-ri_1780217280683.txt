This is a solid, grounded take. You’re focusing on the right constraint (device firmware, not just connectivity), which is where most teams get caught out. A few comments to sharpen it and make it more actionable:
What’s strong
•	You correctly frame the problem as transaction support, not connection plumbing. That’s the key insight.
•	The “three buckets” model is exactly how hardware wallet support plays out in reality.
•	Keeping Xaman as a fallback is pragmatic and avoids blocking your rollout on third parties.
Where it could be tighter
1. Be more explicit about transaction construction vs signing
Right now it slightly blurs two layers:
•	Your app builds the XLS-66 transaction (e.g.  VaultDeposit ).
•	The device only signs a serialized payload.
Important nuance:  
Even if Ledger doesn’t “understand” the transaction, it may still sign it if blind signing is enabled, because it’s just signing bytes.
So the real gating is:
•	Firmware support → needed for clear signing
•	Blind signing → allows early support but weak UX/security
You imply this, but I’d state it more directly so product decisions are clearer.
---
2. Add a product decision layer (not just technical states)
Right now you describe the buckets, but not what you will do in each case. That’s where this becomes actionable.
For example:
•	Clear signing → fully supported, recommended
•	Blind signing → allowed but gated behind:
•	explicit warning
•	maybe an “advanced mode” toggle
•	Not supported → disable + explain
Without this, engineering understands the situation, but product still has ambiguity.
---
3. One missing risk: transaction misrepresentation
You hint at it, but it’s worth calling out more directly:
With blind signing, the user is trusting:
•	your frontend
•	your transaction builder
If either is compromised, funds could be redirected.
That’s not just “weaker UX” — it’s a trust model shift:
•	Clear signing → trust device
•	Blind signing → trust your app
Given your positioning (“verify on your own device”), this is a strategic tension, not just a UX detail.
---
4. Arculus/NFC is underspecified
You mention NFC briefly, but in practice:
•	Web NFC support is limited (especially on desktop)
•	iOS browser support is constrained
•	Most NFC wallet SDKs expect mobile-native flows
So realistically:
•	Ledger = viable web-first path
•	Arculus = likely mobile-only integration initially
That affects roadmap and user expectations.
---
5. Add timeline realism around XLS-66
One subtle but important addition:
Even after XLS-66 passes:
•	Ledger support may lag weeks or months
•	Early users will mostly rely on Xaman
So your launch plan should assume:
•	Day 0: Xaman dominates
•	Ledger direct = staged rollout
---
Suggested refinement (core idea, tightened)
If you wanted to compress your thinking into a sharper internal statement, it would look like:
•	“We can support XLS-66 immediately via Xaman because it abstracts signing.”
•	“Hardware wallets depend on firmware support for clear signing; until then, only blind signing is possible.”
•	“Our product decision is whether we allow blind signing or wait for clear signing per device.”
That framing makes the tradeoffs very explicit.
---
One concrete example (to make it real)
User flow with Ledger before firmware support:
1.	User connects Ledger via WebUSB
2.	Your app builds  VaultDeposit  transaction
3.	Device shows:
•	“Unknown transaction”
•	or raw hex blob
4.	User must enable blind signing
5.	Signs anyway
Compare to after support:
•	Device shows:  
“Deposit 1,000 RLUSD into Vault X”
That difference is exactly why your “three buckets” matter.
